2021年12月25日

Stability of Autonomous System

Consider the autonomous system:
$$
\dot{x}=f(x)
$$
along $f:D\rightarrow R^n$ is locally Lipschitz map from $D\to R^n$. Assume that $f(0)=0$, i.e. , is an equilibrium point.

Definition:

The equilibrium point $x=0$ of (1) is

  • stable: if, for each $\epsilon\gt0$ there is $\delta=\delta(\epsilon)\gt0$, such that $\Vert x(0)\Vert\lt\delta\implies\Vert x(t)\Vert\lt\epsilon,\forall t\ge0$
  • unstable: if it is not stable
  • asymptotically stable: if it is stable and $\delta$ can be chosen such that: $\Vert x(0)\Vert\lt\delta\implies\lim\limits_{t\to\infty}x(t)=0$ .
  • exponentially stable: if there exists two real constants $\alpha,\lambda\gt0$ such that $\Vert x(0)\Vert\lt\delta\implies\Vert x(t)\Vert\lt\alpha\Vert x(0)\Vert e^{-\lambda t},\forall t\ge0$. exponentially $\implies$ asymptotically.

Note that $x(t)\to0$ does not necessarily imply stability: one can construct an example where trajectories converge to the origin, but only after a large detour that violates the stability definition.

Stability of autonomous system.assets\homoclinic_orbit.PNG)

Exanple: Consider $\dot{x}=-(1+\sin^2x)x$ exponentially converge to $x=0$ with a rate $\lambda=1$.

In fact $x(t)=x(0)exp\left(-\int_0^t(1+\sin^2x(\tau))d\tau \right)\implies\Vert x(t)\Vert \le\Vert x(0)\Vert\cdot e^{-t}$.

Example: $\dot{x}=-x^2$ and $x(0)=1$. Its solution is $x(t)=\frac{1}{t+1}$ which is asymptotically stable but not exponentially stable.

  • marginally stable: if $x=0$ is stable but not asymptotically stable.
  • globally asymptotically/exponentially stable: if it holds for all initial states.

Positive Definite Functions–”Energy-Like” Functions

Definition:

A function $V:D\to\mathbb{R}$ is said to be:

  • positive definite if $V(0)=0$ and $V(x)\gt0,\forall x\ne0$
  • positive semidefinite if $V(0)=0$ and $V(x)\ge0,\forall x\ne0$
  • negative definite(resp. negative semi definite) if $-V(x)$ is positive definite(resp. definite semi positive).

Example: The simplest and perhaps the most important class of positive definite functions is a quadratic form:
$$
V(x):R^n\to R=x^TQx,Q\in R^{n\times n},Q=Q^T \notag
$$
V is PD $\Leftrightarrow$ $x^TQx\gt0,\forall x\ne0 \Leftrightarrow\lambda_i(Q)\gt0$, Q is PD

V is PSD $\Leftrightarrow$ $x^TQx\ge0,\forall x\ne0 \Leftrightarrow\lambda_i(Q)\gt0$, Q is PSD

Example: Let $x=\begin{bmatrix}x_1 \ x_2\end{bmatrix}$

$V(x)=x_1^2+x_2^2$ $V(x)=x_1^2$ $V(x)=x_1^2 + x_2^2+1$ $V(x)=\frac{x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}+x_2^2$

$V(x)=\begin{bmatrix}x_1^2 \ x_2^2 \end{bmatrix}$ $V(x)=x^2(4-x^2)$ is PD when $x\in(-2,2)$ and PSD when $x\in[-2,2]$.

Example: Consider the pendulum system with state equations:

Define $x=\begin{bmatrix}\theta \ \dot{\theta} \end{bmatrix}$. State space: $\mathcal{S}^1\times\mathbb{R}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -\frac{k}{m}x_2-\frac{g}{l}\sin x_1
\end{aligned}
$$

one can compute the following energy function(kinetic and potential energy):
$$
\begin{aligned}
V &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2+mgh \
&= \frac{1}{2}m(l\dot{\theta})^2+mgl(1-\cos\theta) \
&= \frac{1}{2}(lx_2)^2+mgl(1-\cos x_1)
\end{aligned}
$$
The derivative of $V$ with respect to $t$ is:
$$
\dot{V} = ml^2x_2\dot{x}_2+mgl\sin x_1\dot{x}_1=-kl^2x_2^2\le0
$$
Canveats:

  1. Positive definite function can be seen as an abstraction of the total “energy” stored in the system
  2. All the Lyapunov stability theorems focus on the study of the time derivative of a positive definite function along the trajectories of the system.

Stability Theorems for Autonomous Systems

Theorem: Lyapunov’s stability theorem

Let $x=0$ be an equilibrium point for $\dot{x}=f(x)$ and $D\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ be a domain containing $x=0$. Let $V: D\to\mathbb{R}$ be a continuously differentiable function such that:

  • $V(0)=0, V(x)\gt0,\forall x\in D-{0}$ which means $V$ is positive definite
  • $\dot{V}(x)\le0,\forall x\in D$ which means $\dot{V}$ is negative semidefinite

Then $x = 0$ is stable. Moreover, if $\dot{V}(x)\lt0,\forall x\in D-{0}$ which meas $\dot{V}$ is negative definite then $x=0$ is asymptotically stable.

Example:

Consider the pendulum example without friction:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -a\sin(x_1)
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Assume the following energy function:
$$
V(x)=a(1-\cos(x_1))+\frac{1}{2}x_2^2 \notag
$$
Clearly $V(0)=0$ and $V(x)\gt0,x\in(-2\pi,2\pi),x_1\ne0$.
$$
\dot{V}(x)=a\sin(x_1)\dot{x}_1+x_2\dot{x}_2=a\sin(x_1)x_2-x_2a\sin(x_1)=0 \notag
$$
Thus the origin is stable. Since $\dot{V}=0$ we can also conclude that the origin is not asymptotically stable.

Next we consider the pendulum example with friction:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -a\sin(x_1)-bx_2
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Consider:
$$
V(x)=a(1-\cos(x_1))+\frac{1}{2}x_2^2
\notag
$$
Then $\dot{V}=-bx_2^2\le0$ which is negative semidefinite. We can only conclude that the origin is stable. But actually the system is asymptotically stable. The chosen Lyapunov function candidate fails to show this fact.

Replace the term $\frac{1}{2}x^2$ by the more general quadratic form $\frac{1}{2}x^TPx$ for some $2\times2$ positive definite matrix $P$.

We define:
$$
\begin{aligned}
V &= \frac{1}{2}x^TPx+a(1-\cos x_1) \
&=\frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}x_1 & x_2\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}p_{11} & p_{12} \ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}x_1 \ x_2\end{bmatrix}
+a(1-\cos x_1)
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
where $p_{11}\gt0$ and $p_{11}\cdot p_{22}-p_{12}^2\gt0$. The time derivative along the system is:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}(x)&=\frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}x_1 & x_2\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}p_{11} & p_{12} \ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}\dot{x}1 \ \dot{x}2\end{bmatrix}
+a\sin x_1\cdot x_2 \
&=\begin{bmatrix}x_1 & x_2\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}p{11} & p{12} \ p_{12} & p_{22} \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}x_2 \ -a\sin x_1-bx_2\end{bmatrix}
+a\sin x_1\cdot x_2 \
&=p_{11}\cdot x_1x_2-p_{12}x_1(a\sin x_1+bx_2)+p_{12}x_2^2-p_{22}x_2[a\sin x_1+bx_2]
+a\sin x_1\cdot x_2 \
&=(p_{11}-p_{12}b)x_1x_2-p_{12}a\cdot x_1\sin x_1-(p_{22}b-p_{12})x_2^2+(1-p_{22})a\sin x_1\cdot x_2
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
In order to let $p_{11}=p_{12}b$ and $p_{22}b-p_{12}\gt0,p_{22}=1$, we can choose: $p_{22}=1,p_{12}=\frac{b}{2},p_{11}=\frac{b^2}{2}$. We have:
$$
\dot{V}=-\frac{ab}{2}x_1\cdot\sin x_1-\frac{b}{2}x_2^2\le0
$$
when $x_1\in(-\pi,\pi)$, the system is negative definite. Thus we can conclude that the origin is asymptotically stable.

This example emphasizes an important feature: the Lyapunov theorem;s conditions are only sufficient

Example:

Let’s study the following nonlinear system:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1&=x_1(x_1^2+x_2^2-2)-4x_1x_2^2 \
\dot{x}_2&=x_2(x_1^2+x_2^2-2)+4x_1^2x_2 \
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
with the equilibrium point at the origin. Consider the following positive definite function:
$$
V = \frac{1}{2}(x_1^2+x_2^2) \notag
$$
Its derivative along the system is:
$$
\dot{V} = x_1\dot{x}_1+x_2\dot{x}_2=(x_1^2+x_2^2)(x_1^2+x_2^2-2)
$$
$\dot{V}$ is negative definite over the ball $B_r={x:x_1^2+x_2^2\lt2}$. Therefore, the origin is locally asymptotically stable.

Theorem-Globally Asymptotically Stable(GAS)

Let $x=0$ be an equilibrium point for $\dot{x}=f(x)$. Let $V:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ be a continuously differentiable function such that:

  • $V(0)=0$ and $V(x)\gt0,\forall x\ne0$( PD)
  • $\Vert x\Vert\to\infty\implies V(x)\to\infty$ (radially unbounded,径向无界)
  • $\dot{V}(x)\lt0,\forall x\ne0$(ND)

Then $x=0$ is globally asymptotically stable(GAS)

Example:$V(x)=x_1^2+x_2^2$ is radically unbounded and $V(x)=\frac{x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}+x_2^2$ is not radically unbounded

Global Asymptotic stability:

Consider $V(x)=\frac{x_1^2}{1+x_1^2}+x_2^2$.

Stability of autonomous system.assets/gloablStability.PNG)

Caveat: The reason for radial boundedness condition is to assure that the contour curves $V(x)=V_\alpha$ correspond to closed curves. If the curves are not closed, it is possible for state trajectories to drift away from the equilibrium point, even though the state keeps going through contours corresponding to smaller and smaller $V_\alpha$’s.

The Invariance Principle(不变集原理)

Definition:

A set $M$ is an invariant set with respect to $\dot{x}=f(x)$ if
$$
x(0)\in M\implies x(t)\in M,\forall t\in R \notag
$$
A set $M$ is a positively invariant set with respect to $\dot{x}=f(x)$ if:
$$
x(0)\in M\implies x(t)\in M,\forall t\ge0 \notag
$$
$x(t)$ approaches a set $M$ as $t\to\infty$ if (有限时间内距离小于任意正值)
$$
\forall\epsilon\gt0,\exists T\gt0:dist(x(t),M)\lt\epsilon,\forall t\gt T\notag
$$
where $dist(p,M)=\inf_{x\in M}\Vert p-x\Vert$. (边界严格定义)

Example:

  1. Equilibrium Points are invariant set.
  2. the whole space $R^n$
  3. $\Omega_c={x\in R^n\vert V(x)\le C }$ with $\dot{V}\lt0$.
  4. limit cycle (极限环)

A trajectory of a system is called close if it is not a constant but returns to its starting point, i.e., there exists some $t_0\gt0$ such that $x(t+t_0)=x(t)$. An isolated closed orbit is called a limit cycle.

There are three types of limit cycles:

  1. Stable Limit Cycle: all trajectories in vicinity(邻域) of the limit cycle converge to it as $t\to\infty$;
  2. Unstable Limit Cycle: all trajectories in vicinity of the limit cycle diverge to it as $t\to\infty$;
  3. Semi-stable Limit Cycle: some trajectories in vicinity of the limit cycle converge to it, while others diverge from it as $t\to\infty$.

Theorem-La Salle’s Theorem

Let

  • $\Omega\subset D$ be a compact positively invariant set.
  • $V:D\to\mathbb{R}$ be a continuously differentiable function such that $\dot{V}(x)\le0$ in $\Omega$.
  • $E={x\in\Omega:\dot{V}=0 }$.
  • $M$ be the largest invariant set in $E$. ($M\subseteq E$ and can stand identically in itself,)

Then every solution starting in $\Omega$ approaches $M$ as $t\to\infty$.

Example:

Consider the pendulum example without friction:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -a\sin(x_1),a\gt0
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Differentiable function $V=\frac{1}{2}x^2_2+a(1-\cos x_1)$ with $\dot{V}=0$

We chose $\Omega={x\vert V(x)\le C}$, $E={x\vert \dot{V}=0,x\in\Omega }$ thus $E=\Omega$ and $M=\Omega$.

Consider the pendulum example with friction:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -a\sin(x_1)-bx_2
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Differentiable function $\dot{V}=-bx_2^2\le0$.

We chose $\Omega={x\vert V(x)\le C}$, $E={x\vert\dot{V}(x)=0,x\in\Omega}={x_2=0,x_1\in\Omega }$. Thus $M={(0,0)}$

Remark: Besides often yielding conclusions on asymptotic stability when $\dot{V}$ is only negative semi-definite, the invariant set theorem also allow us to extend the concept of Lyapunov function so as to describe convergence to dynamic behaviors more general than equilibrium, e.g., convergence to a limit cycle.

When $E$ is the origin ?

Corollary 4.1

Let $x=0$ be an equilibrium point of $\dot{x}=f(x)$. Let $V:D\to\mathbb{R}$ be a $C^1$ positive definite function containing the origin $x\equiv0$ such that $\dot{V}\le0$ in $D$. Let $S={x\in D:\dot{V}=0 }$ and suppose that no solution can stay identically in $S$, other than the trivial solution $x(t)=0$. Then, origin is asymptotically stable.

Corollary 4.2

Let $x=0$ be an equilibrium point of $\dot{x}=f(x)$. Let $V:D\to\mathbb{R}$ be a $C^1$, radically unbounded, positive definite function such that $\dot{V}\le0$ for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$. Let $S={x\in\mathbb{R}:\dot{V}=0 }$ and suppose that no solution can stay identically in $S$, other than the trivial solution $x(t)=0$. Then $x=0$ is GAS.

Examples

Example: Still consider pendulum example with friction, chose $S={x\in D\vert\dot{V}\equiv0}={x_2\equiv0}$. From $x_2\equiv0\implies\dot{x}_2=0\implies a\sin x_1\equiv0\implies x_1\equiv0$, use corollary 4.1 can prove the asymptotically stable of the origin.

Example: Consider the following first-order system:
$$
\dot{y}=ay+u
\notag
$$
together with the adaptive control law
$$
u=-k(t)y,\dot{k}=\gamma y^2,\gamma\gt0
\notag
$$
Taking $x_1=y,x_2=k$, we can obtain:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= ax_1-x_1x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= \gamma x_1^2
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
The line $x_1=0$ is an equilibrium set. We want to show that the trajectories approach this equilibrium set as $t\to\infty$, which means the adaptive controller regulates $y\to0$.

Consider the following Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V(x)=\frac{1}{2}x_1^2 + \frac{1}{2\gamma}(x-k_0)^2
\notag
$$
where $k_0\gt a$ is a constant. The derivative of $V$ along the trajectories is given by:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}(x) &= x_1\cdot\dot{x}_1+\frac{1}{\gamma}(x_2-k_0)\cdot\dot{x}_2 \
&= ax_1^2-x_1^2x_2+(x_2-k_0)\cdot x_1^2 \
&= -(k_0-a)x_1^2\le0
\end{aligned}
$$
Step 1 : Define the set $\Omega_c={x\in R^2\vert V(x)\le C}$, which is an invariant set.

Step 2: The set $E={x\in\Omega_c\vert\dot{V}(x)\equiv0}={x\in\Omega_c\vert x_1=0}$

Step 3: The largest invariant set $M=E$. From La Salle’s Theorem, we can conclude that $\lim \limits_{t\to\infty}x_1(t)=0$. Moreover, since $V(x)$ is radially unbounded, the conclusion is global.

Example:

Consider the following nonlinear system:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 + x_1(\beta^2-x_1^2-x_2^2) \
\dot{x}_2 &= x_1 + x_1(\beta^2-x_1^2-x_2^2)
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
The origin is an equilibrium point. Also, the set of points defined by the circle $x_1^2+x_2^2=\beta$ continue an invariant set.

Solution 1: Assume that $r=\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2},\theta=\arctan\frac{x_2}{x_1}$. We have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{r} &= \frac{1}{2}(x_1^2+x_2^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}(2x_1\dot{x}_1+2x_2\dot{x}_2)
=\frac{1}{r}r^2(\beta^2-r^2)=r(\beta^2-r^2) \
\dot{\theta} &= \frac{1}{1+(\frac{x_2}{x_1})^2}\cdot\frac{\dot{x}_2x_1-\dot{x}_1x_2}{x_1^2}=\frac{-r^2}{r^2}=-1
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Solution 2: We now investigate the stability of the limit cycle using La Salle’s Theorem. To this end, consider the following function (not PD function):
$$
V(x) = \frac{1}{4}(x_1^2+x_2^2-\beta^2)^2
\notag
$$
which represents a measure of the “distance” to the limit cycle. Then we have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}(x) &= \frac{1}{2}(x_1^2+x_2^2-\beta^2)(2x_1\dot{x}_1+2x_2\dot{x}_2) \
&= -(x_1^2+x_2^2-\beta^2)^2(x_1^2+x_2^2)\le0
\end{aligned}
$$
Step 1: Chose $\Omega={x\in R^2\vert V(x)\le C}$

Step 2: Find $E={x\in\Omega_c\vert\dot{V}(x)=0}$ Clearlay $E={x\vert x_1^2+x_2^2=\beta^2}\cup{(0,0)}$.

Step 3: Find $M$, the largest invariant set in $E$.

$M=E$. Thus choosing $C$ such that $0\lt C\lt\frac{1}{4}\beta^4$. $\Omega_c={x\in R^2,V(x)\le C}$ includes the limit cycle but not the origin. The application of La Salle’s theorem with $C\in(0,\frac{1}{4}\beta^4)$ shows that every motion starting in $\Omega_c$ converges to the limit cycle, and therefore the limit cycle is stable.

Assume $C=\frac{1}{4}\beta^4-\epsilon$. $\forall x$ satisfies $V(x)\le C$ will converges to the limit cycle, thus the origin is unstable.

Linear Systems and Linearization

Consider the time-invariant linear system: $\dot{x}=Ax$

Caveats: Some reasons for investigating stability of LTI systems via Lyapunov method:

  1. The Lyapunov analysis permits studying linear and nonlinear systems under the same framework, where LTI is a special case.
  2. We will introduce a very useful class of Lyapunov functions that appears frequently in the literature.
  3. We will study the stability of nonlinear systems via linearization of the state equation and try to get some insights into the limitations associated with this process.

Consider a quadratic Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V(x)=x^TPx
\notag
$$
where $P\in R^{n\times n}$ is positive definite. The derivative along LTI system:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}(x) &= x^TA\dot{x}+\dot{x}^TPx \
&=x^TPAx+(Ax)^TPx \
&=x^TPAx+x^TA^TPx \
&= x^T(PA+A^TP)x\triangleq -x^TQx
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
where $Q$ is a symmetric matrix defined by $PA+A^TP=-Q$ called (Lyapunov equation).

Lypunov Theorem

A matrix $A$ is Hurwitz, that is, $Re(\lambda_i)\lt0$ for all eigenvalues of $A$, if and only if, for any given $Q=Q^T\gt0$ there exists a $P=P^T\gt0$ that satisfies the Lyapunov equation:
$$
PA+A^TP=-Q
$$
Moreover, if $A$ is Hurwitz, then $P$ is the unique solution (9).

When it comes to nonlinear system $\dot{x}=f(x)$, we using linearization. We have:
$$
\dot{x}=f(x)\implies\dot{x}=Ax+g(x)
\notag
$$
where $A$ is Jacobian matrix:
$$
A=\frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}\bigg|{x=0}
=\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} &\cdots &\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \
\vdots & & \vdots \
\frac{\partial f_n}{\partial x_1} & \cdots &\frac{\partial f_n}{\partial x}
\end{bmatrix}\bigg|{x=0}
$$
and $\frac{\Vert g(x)\Vert}{\Vert x\Vert}\to0$ as $\Vert x\Vert\to0$.

Consider the following Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V(x)=x^TPx \notag
$$
The derivative of $V(x)$ along the trajectories is given by:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}(x) &= x^TA\dot{x}+\dot{x}^TPx \
&=x^TP(Ax+g(x))+(Ax+g(x))^TPx \
&=x^TPAx+x^TA^TPx \
&= x^T(PA+A^TP)x + x^TPg(x)+g^T(x)Px \
&= -x^TQx+2xPg(x)
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
Since $A$ is Hurwitz, $x^TQx\gt0$. Regarding the other term, note that:
$$
\frac{\norm{g(x)}_2}{\norm{x}_2}\to0\ as\ \norm{x}_2\to0
\notag
$$
Therefore $\forall R\gt0,\exist r$ such that $\frac{\norm{g(x)}_2}{\norm{x}_2}\lt R,\forall\norm{x}_2\lt r$. Hence :
$$
\dot{V}(x)\le-x^TQx+2\norm{x}_2\norm{p}_2\norm{g(x)}_2\le-x^TQx+2\norm{p}_2R\norm{x}_2^2
\notag
$$
When $\norm{x}_2\lt r$ Note that:
$$
\lambda_\min(Q)\norm{x}^2_2\le x^TQx\le\lambda_\max(Q)\norm{x}_2^2
\notag
$$
It follows that:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V}&\le-\lambda_\min(Q)\norm{x}^2_2+2\gamma\norm{p}_2\cdot\norm{x}_2^2 \
&=-(\lambda_\min(Q)-2R\norm{p}_2)\cdot\norm{x}_2^2
\end{aligned}
$$
Choosing $r$ such that $\lambda_\min(Q)\gt 2R\norm{p}_2$, $\dot{V}$ is negative definite. Therefore $x=0$ is locally asymptotically stable.

Theorem-Lyapunov’s indirect method

Let $x=0$ be an equilibrium point for the nonlinear system $\dot{x}=f(x)$ where $f:D\to\R^n$ is $C^1$ and $D$ is a neighborhood of the origin. Let $A=\frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}$

Then,

  1. The origin is asymptotically stable if $Re(\lambda_i)\lt0$ for all eigenvalues of $A$
  2. The origin is unstable if $Re(\lambda_i)\gt0$ for one ore more eigenvalues of $A$

We have no conclusion for some $Re(\lambda_i)=0$ in nonlinear system.

Example:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x}_1 &= x_2 \
\dot{x}_2 &= -a\sin(x_1)-bx_2
\end{aligned}
\notag
$$
It has two equilibrium points: $(0,0)$ and $(\pi,0)$.
