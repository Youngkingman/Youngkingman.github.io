Adaptive Control

Basic Steps

  • Characterize the desired behavior of the closed-loop systems
  • Determine a suitable control law containing adjustable parameters
  • find a mechanism(an adaptation law) for adjusting those parameters
  • Analyze the convergence properties and implement the control law

Some approaches

Gain Scheduling

Self-Tuning Controller(STC)

  • Combines a controller with an on-line plant parameter estimator
  • Performs simultaneous (实时) parameters identification and control
  • Uses Certainty Equivalence Principle: controller parameters are computed from the estimates of the plant parameters as if they were the true ones

Model Reference Adaptive Control(MRAC)

  • Plan: containing unknown parameters and having a known structure
  • Reference model: specifying the desired output of the control system
  • Feedback control law: containing adjustable parameters
  • Adaptation mechanism: updating the adjustable parameters

Indirect and Direct Adaptive Control

Indirected Adaptive Control:

The process model and possible the disturbance characteristics are first determined. The controller parameters are designed on the basis of this information.

Directed Adaptive Control:

The controller parameters are changed directly without the characteristics of the process and its disturbances first being determined

*Some Examples of Adaptive Control

Consider the following first-order scalar system:
$$
\dot{x}=ax+u
$$
where $a$ is an unknown parameter, $x$ is the state and $u$ is the control input.

Control Object: Design $u$, such that all signals in the closed-loop system are bounded and $x$ tracks the state $x_{ref}$ of the following reference model given by:
$$
\dot{x}{ref}=-a{ref}x_{ref}+b_{ref}u_c
$$
where $a_{ref} \gt0$(Input-to-State Stable) and $b_{ref}$ are unknown parameters, and $u_c(t)$ is the reference input command which is bounded and piecewise continuous.

We proposed the control law:
$$
u^*=k_1^x+k_2^u_c
$$
such that:
$$
\dot{x}=(a+k_1^*)x+k_2^u_c
$$
If $a+k_1^=-a_{ref}$ and $k_2^*=b_{ref}$, with $e=x-x_{ref}$, we have:
$$
\dot{e}=-a_{ref}e
$$
Since $a$ is unknown, $k_1^*$ cannot be implemented. Then we design the control law
$$
u=k_1(t)x+b_{ref}u_c
$$

  • Direct Adaptive Control: what is $\dot{k}_1(t)$ like ?
  • Indirect Adaptive Control: we got $k_1(t)=-\hat{a}(t)-a_{ref}$, what is $\dot{\hat{a}}(t)$ like?

Direct MRAC Design for Scalar System

Considering system:
$$
\dot{x}=ax+bu
$$
where $a$ and $b$ are unknown parameters but the sign of $b$ is known. Construct the same reference model given by(2), we have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x} &= ax+b(k_1^x+k_2^u_c) \
&=(a+bk_1^*)x+k_2^u_c
\end{aligned}
$$
Then the perfect model following condition as follow:
$$
k_1^=\frac{-a-a_{ref}}{b},k_2^*=\frac{b_{ref}}{b}
$$
Since $a$,$b$ are unknown, the controller(3) cannot be implemented. Therefore, we propose the following control law with adjustable parameters:
$$
u=\hat{k_1}(t)x+\hat{k_2}(t)u_c(t)
$$
there $\hat{k}1$ and $\hat{k}2$ are the estimates of $k_1^*$ and $k_2^*$ respectively. We substitute(10) into (7):
$$
\dot{x}=(a+b\hat{k}1(t))x+\hat{k}2(t)u_c(t)
$$
and rewrite the latter by using the perfect model following conditions(9):
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x} &= (a+b\hat{k}1(t))x+\hat{k}2(t)u_c(t)-a{ref}x + b{ref}u_c+a{ref}x-b{ref} \
&=-a{ref}x + b{ref}u_c+(a_{ref}+a+b\hat{k}1)x + (b\hat{k}2-b{ref})u_c \
&=-a{ref}x+b_{ref}u_c+b(\hat{k}1-k_1^*)x+b(\hat{k}2-k_2^*)u_c \
&=-a{ref}x+b{ref}u_c+b\tilde{k}_1(t)x+b\tilde{k}_2(t)u_c
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\tilde{k}_1(t)=\hat{k}_1-k_1^*$ and $\tilde{k}_2(t)=\hat{k}_2-k_2^*$.

Define the tracking error:
$$
e :=x(t)-x_{ref}(t)
$$
Then from (14) and (2), the error dynamics is:
$$
\dot{e}=\dot{x}-\dot{x}{ref}=-a{ref}e+b\tilde{k}_1(t)x+b\tilde{k}_2(t)u_c
$$
Consider a Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V=\frac{1}{2}e^2+\frac{\vert b\vert}{2\gamma_1}\tilde{k}_1^2+\frac{\vert b\vert}{2\gamma_2}\tilde{k}_2^2
$$
The time derivative of $V$ along(16) can be written as:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &= e\dot{e}+\tilde{k}_1\dot{\tilde{k}}_1|b|/\gamma_1+\tilde{k}_2\dot{\tilde{k}}2|b|/\gamma_2 \
&=-a{ref}e^2+\frac{|b|}{\gamma_1}\tilde{k}_1(\dot{\hat{k}}_1+\gamma_1\cdot sgn(b)xe)+\frac{|b|}{\gamma_2}\tilde{k}_2(\dot{\hat{k}}_2+\gamma_2\cdot sgn(b)u_ce)
\end{aligned}
$$
We can choose:
$$
\dot{\hat{k}_1}=-\gamma_1\cdot sgn(b)xe\ \dot{\hat{k}2}=-\gamma_2\cdot sgn(b)u_ce
$$
which leads to
$$
\dot{V}=-a{ref}e^2\le0 \notag
$$
Thus $V(t)\le V(0)$. From(31), $e,\tilde{k}_1,\tilde{k}2$ are bounded and $x$ is bounded. From(30), $\dot{e}$ is bounded. Then $\ddot{V}$ is also bounded. We can conclude from Barbalat’s Lemma that $\lim \limits{t\rightarrow \infty}e(t)=0$.

summary

  • Find a controller structure(based on known parameters)
  • Derive the error dynamics
  • Design a suitable Lyapunov function
  • Derive parameter updating laws such that $\dot{V}\le 0$

Direct MRAC Design for Scalar System

Consider the following first-order scalar system:
$$
\dot{x}=ax+b(u+f(x))
$$
where $f(x)=\phi(x)\theta$ with $\phi$ begin a bounded and continuous known function, and $a,b,\theta$ are unknown parameters but the sign of $b$ is known. The reference model is still given by
$$
\dot{x}{ref}=-a{ref}x_{ref}+b_{ref}u_c
$$
We proposed the control law:
$$
u(t)=\hat{k}1(t)x+\hat{k}2(t)u_c(t)-\phi(x)\hat{\theta}(t)
$$
Then we have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x} &= -a{ref}x+b{ref}u_c+b\tilde{k}1(t)x+b\tilde{k}2(t)u_c-b(\hat{\theta}-\theta)\phi(x) \
&= -a{ref}x+b{ref}u_c+b\tilde{k}_1(t)x+b\tilde{k}_2(t)u_c-b\tilde{\theta}\phi(x) \
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\tilde{k}_1(t)=\hat{k}_1-k_1^*$ , $\tilde{k}_2(t)=\hat{k}_2-k_2^*$ and $\tilde{\theta}=\hat{\theta}(t)-\theta$.

Then the error dynamics is
$$
\dot{e}=-a_{ref}e+b\tilde{k}_1(t)x+b\tilde{k}_2(t)u_c-b\tilde{\theta}\phi(x)
$$
Consider the following Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V=\frac{1}{2}e^2+\frac{|b|}{2\gamma_1}\tilde{k}_1^2
+\frac{|b|}{2\gamma_2}\tilde{k}2^2
+\frac{|b|}{2\gamma_3}\tilde{\theta}^2
$$
Its time derivative is:
$$
\dot{V}=-a{ref}e^2
+\frac{|b|}{\gamma_1}\tilde{k}_1(\dot{\hat{k}}_1+\gamma_1\cdot sgn(b)xe)
+\frac{|b|}{\gamma_2}\tilde{k}_2(\dot{\hat{k}}_2+\gamma_2\cdot sgn(b)u_ce)\
+\frac{|b|}{\gamma_3}\tilde{\theta}(\dot{\hat{\theta}}-\gamma_3\cdot sgn(b)\phi(x)e)
$$
We can choose:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{\hat{k}_1} &= -\gamma_1\cdot sgn(b)xe \
\dot{\hat{k}_2} &= -\gamma_2\cdot sgn(b)u_ce \
\dot{\hat{\theta}} &= \gamma_3\cdot sgn(b)\phi(x)e
\end{aligned}
$$
Tips(important）:

Consider a system different from(43) like
$$
\dot{x} = ax + bu+\phi\theta \
$$
$a,b,\theta$ are unknown. We should transfer the system into the following form:
$$
\dot{x}=ax+b(u+\phi(x)\theta_1)=ax+b(u+\frac{\theta}{b}\phi(x))
$$
Then the previous MRAC method design with nonlinear term can be applied to this problem.

Indirect MRAC Design for Scalar System

In directed adaptive control, $\hat{k}1(t)$ and $\hat{k}2(t)$ are generated directly by adaptation laws. In indirect adaptive control, we follow a different approach. We calculate $\hat{k}1(t)$ and $\hat{k}2(t)$ by using the perfect model following condition and the estimates $\hat{a}(t)$ and $\hat{b}(t)$ of unknown parameters $a,b$ as :
$$
\hat{k}1(t)=\frac{-\hat{a}-a{ref}}{\hat{b}} \
\hat{k}2(t)=\frac{b{ref}}{\hat{b}}
$$
which implies that the controller:
$$
u=\hat{k_1}(t)x+\hat{k_2}(t)u_c(t)=\frac{1}{\hat{b}}[-(\hat{a}+a{ref})x+b{ref}u_c]
$$
Using controller(56), the closed-loop system is:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x} &= (a-\hat{a}+\hat{a})x+(b-\hat{b}+\hat{b})u \
&=-(\hat{a}-a)x-(\hat{b}-b)u+\hat{a}x+\hat{b}u \
&= -(\hat{a}-a)x-(\hat{b}-b)u+\hat{a}x+\hat{b}u \
&= -a{ref}x+b{ref}u_c-\tilde{a}x-\tilde{b}u
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\tilde{a}=\hat{a}-a$,$\tilde{b}=\hat{b}-b$.

Then the error dynamics can be written as:
$$
\dot{e}=-a_{ref}e-\tilde{a}x-\tilde{b}u
$$
Consider the following Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V=\frac{1}{2}e^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma_1}\tilde{a}^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma_2}\tilde{b}^2, \gamma_1,\gamma_2 \gt 0
$$
The time derivative of $V$ along $e$ is given by:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &= e\dot{e}
+ \frac{1}{\gamma_1}\tilde{a}\dot{\hat{a}}
+ \frac{1}{\gamma_2}\tilde{b}\dot{\hat{b}} \
&=-a_{ref}e^2
+\frac{\tilde{a}}{\gamma_1}(\dot{\hat{a}}-\gamma_1xe)
+\frac{\tilde{b}}{\gamma_2}(\dot{\hat{b}}-\gamma_2ue)
\end{aligned}
$$
In order to yield $\dot{V} \le 0$, we choose:
$$
\dot{\hat{a}}=\gamma_1xe\
\dot{\hat{b}}=\gamma_2ue
$$
Then $\dot{V}=-a_{ref}e^2 \le 0$, which implies that $V(t)\le V(0)$, $e,\tilde{a},\tilde{b}$ are all bounded.

In order to claim the boundedness of $u$, we need to modify the adaptation law(61) and prevent $\hat{b}(t)$ from going through zero. Such a modification can be achieved using the following a prior knowedge:
Assumption: The $sgn(b)$ and a lower bound $b_{min} \gt 0$ for $|b|$ are known.

Let us consider the following modification of $\dot{\hat{b}}$ in (61)
$$
\dot{\hat{b}}=
\left{
\begin{aligned}
&\gamma_2eu,\ |\hat{b}|\gt b_{min}\ or\ |\hat{b}|=b_{min}\ and\ eu\cdot sgn(b) \gt 0 \
&0,\ otherwise(|\hat{b}|=b_{min}\ and\ eu\cdot sgn(b) \lt0)
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$
The main motivation here is to stop adaptation of $\hat{b}$ if the parameter reaches its lower absolute limit value $b_{min}$ with a nonzero time derivative.

We need to argue that the modification(62) does indeed prevent $\hat{b}(t)$ from going through zero and at the same time, it preserves $\dot{V} \le 0$. For this to be true, it is sufficient to show that:
$$
\tilde{b}(\dot{\hat{b}}-\gamma_2ue) \le 0
$$
We check $-\tilde{b}ue \le 0$ when $\dot{\hat{b}}=0,|\hat{b}|=b_{min}$ and $eu\cdot sgn(b) \lt0$.

Notice that $-\tilde{b}ue=-(\hat{b}-b)ue\cdot sgn(b)\cdot sgn(b)$.

  • $sgn(b)\gt0,\hat{b}=b_{min} \le b \Rightarrow \hat{b}-b \le 0\Rightarrow -\tilde{b}ue\le0$
  • $sgn(b)\lt0,\hat{b}=-b_{min} \ge b \Rightarrow \hat{b}-b \ge 0\Rightarrow -\tilde{b}ue\ge0$

Overall, we can get that under the modification(62), inequality (63) holds, thus the following inequality holds:
$$
\dot{V} \le -a_{ref}e^2 \le 0
$$
And thus $e,\tilde{a},\tilde{b}$ are bounded. Since $x_{ref},u_c$ are bounded, $x,u,\dot{e}$ are all bounded. From LaSalle Yoshizawa Theorem $\lim \limits_{t\rightarrow \infty} e(t)=0$.

Remark: Another Proof

Integrating both sides of (64) yields:
$$
\begin{aligned}
&V(t)-V(0) \le -a_{ref}\int_0^t e^2(\tau)d\tau \le 0 \
&\Rightarrow \int_0^te^2(\tau)d\tau \le\frac{V(0)-V(t)}{a_{ref}} \le\frac{V(0)}{a_{ref}} \
&\Rightarrow e \in \mathcal{L}_2
\end{aligned}
$$
Furthermore, from the above analysis we have $e,\dot{e} \in \mathcal{L}_\infty$. From Barbalat’s Lemma we have the same conclusion.

Direct MRAC Design for MIMO Systems

In this section, we will extend applicability of the MRAC design from scalar dynamics to multi-input multi-output nonlinear systems in the form:
$$
\dot{x}=Ax+B\Lambda u
$$
where $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ is the system state, $u\in\mathbb{R}^m$ is the control input, and $B\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$ is the known control matrix, while $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and $\Lambda\in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ are unknown constant matrices. In addition, it is assumed that $\Lambda$ is diagonal, its elements $\lambda_i$ are strictly positive and the pair $(A,B\Lambda)$ is controllable. The uncertainty in $\Lambda$ is introduced to model control failures or modeling errors, in the sense that there may exist uncertain control gains or the designer may have incorrectly estimated the system control effectiveness.

Control Objective: Design $u$ such that all signals in the closed-loop system are bounded and $x$ tracks the state $x_{ref}$ of the following reference model given by:
$$
\dot{x}{ref}=-A{ref}x_{ref}+B_{ref}u_c
$$
where $A_{ref} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is Hurwitz, $B_{ref}\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$ and $u_c(t) \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the external bounded command vector.

If the matrices $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and $\Lambda\in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ were known, we can apply the control law:
$$
u = K_1^x+K_2^u_c
$$
where $K_1^* \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$, $K_2^* \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ and we obtain:
$$
\dot{x}=(A+B\Lambda K_1^*)x+B\Lambda K_2^u_c
$$
In general, there is no guarantee that the ideal gain $K_1^$ and $K_2^*$ exist such that:
$$
A+B\Lambda K_1^*=A_{ref} \
B\Lambda K_2^* = B_{ref}
$$
However, in practice, if the structure of $A$ and $B$ are known, $A_{ref}$ and $B_{ref}$ may be designed such that (70) has a solution. Let us assume that $K_1^*$ and $K_2^*$ in (70) exist. Then we proposed the following control law:
$$
u=\hat{K}1(t)x+\hat{K}2(t)u_c
$$
By adding and subtracting the desired terms, we obtain:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{x} &= A{ref}x+B{ref}u_c-A_{ref}x-B_{ref} + Ax

  • B\Lambda \hat{K}1(t)x+B\Lambda \hat{K}2(t)u_c \
    &=A    {ref}x+B    {ref}u_c

+(A+B\Lambda\hat{K}1(t)-A{ref})x +(B\Lambda \hat{K}2(t)-B{ref})u_c \
&=A_{ref}x+B_{ref}u_c
+B\Lambda \tilde{K}_1(t)x + B\Lambda \tilde{K}_2(t)u_c \
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\tilde{K}_1:=\hat{K}_1-K_1^*$ and $\tilde{K}2:=\hat{K}2-K_2^*$. Define the tracking error $e := x-x{ref}$. We can obtain the dynamics of $e$ :
$$
\dot{e}=A{ref}e+B\Lambda \tilde{K}_1(t)x+B\Lambda\tilde{K}_2(t)u_c
$$
Considering the following Lyapunov candidate:
$$
V=\frac{1}{2}e^2
+\frac{1}{2}\tr\left{\tilde{K}_1^T\tilde{K}_1\right}
+\frac{1}{2}\tr\left {\tilde{K}_2^T\tilde{K}_2\right}
$$
*Use sum of every column vector’s inner product makes sense here, still this Lyapunov candidate can not lead to asymptotically stable.

Since $A_{ref}$ is Hurwitz, we can get from Lyapunov’s Theorem that for any positive definite $Q\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ there exists a unique positive definite $P \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ such that:
$$
A_{ref}^TP + PA_{ref}=-Q \lt 0
$$
We then consider the following Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V=e^TPe
+\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_1\Lambda\tilde{K}_1 \right}
+\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_2\Lambda\tilde{K}_2 \right}
$$
Its derivative along (73) can be written as:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} =& \dot{e}^TPe+e^TP\dot{e}
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_1\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}1 \right}
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_2\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}2 \right} \
=&(A{ref}e)^TPe+e^TP(A{ref}e)+2e^TPB\Lambda\tilde{K}_1x+\cdots \
&+2e^TPB\Lambda\tilde{K}_2u_c
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_1\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}_1 \right}
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_2\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}_2 \right} \
=&-e^TQe
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_1\Lambda B^TPex^T \right}
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_2\Lambda B^TPeu_c^T\right} +\cdots\
&+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_1\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}_1 \right}
+2\tr\left{\tilde{K}^T_2\Lambda\dot{\hat{K}}_2 \right} \
\end{aligned}
$$
And the adaptation updating laws can be designed as:
$$
\dot{\hat{K}}_1=-B^TPex^T,\ \dot{\hat{K}}_2=-B^TPeu_c^T
$$
Then we have $\dot{V}=-e^TQe \le 0$.

Thus $V(t) \le V(0)$, which implies that $e,\tilde{K}1,\tilde{K}2$ are bounded. Since $A{ref}$ is Hurwitz and $u_c$ is bounded, from ISS theorem, $x{ref}$ is also bounded, which implies that $x$ is bounded. From errors dynamics (73), $\dot{e}$ is bounded. Then $\ddot{V}=-2e^TQ\dot{e}$ is bounded. From Barbalat;s lemma $\lim \limits_{t\rightarrow \infty}\dot{V}(t)=0$,i.e., $\lim \limits_{t\rightarrow\infty}e(t)=0$.

Nussbaum gains

Consider the following system:
$$
\dot{x}=ax+bu
$$
where $a$ and $b$ are unknown constants and $b\ne0$ but unlike previous discussion the direction of $b$ is unknown. Use control law $u = \hat{k}(t)x$, the closed-loop system is :
$$
\dot{x}=(a+b\hat{k}(t))x
$$
Define ideal gain $u=k^x$ with $a+bk^:=k_0\le0$, then system(82) can be written as:
$$
\dot{x}=k_0x+b(\hat{k}(t)-k^*)x=k_0x+b\tilde{k}(t)x
$$
Consider a Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
V=\frac{1}{2}x^2+\frac{b}{\gamma}\tilde{k}^2(t)
$$
It’s derivative along (82) is :
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &= x\dot{x}+\frac{|b|}{\gamma}\tilde{k}(t)\dot{\hat{k}}(t) \
&= k_0x^2+b\tilde{k}(t)x^2
+\frac{|b|}{\gamma}\tilde{k}(t)\dot{\hat{k}}(t)
\end{aligned}
$$
Design $\dot{\hat{k}}(t)=-\gamma\cdot sgn(b)x^2$ is not implementable. So we can use a Nussbaum gain to take the place of $\hat{k}(t)$.

Nussbaum gains
$$
\mathcal{N}(k)=k^2\cos k
$$
is one kind of Nussbaum gain. It satisfies the following condition:

  • $\limsup_{s\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{s}\int_0^s\mathcal{N}(\tau)d\tau=+\infty$
  • $\liminf_{s\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{s}\int_0^s\mathcal{N}(\tau)d\tau=-\infty$

$\mathcal{N}(k)$ changes its sign an infinite number of times as $k \rightarrow \infty$.

Other Nussbaum gains: $k^2\sin k$, $e^{k^2}\sin k$,…

By using nussbaum gain in the form of (86), the closed-loop system(82) is:
$$
\dot{x}=(a+b\mathcal{N}(k))x=(a+bk^2\cos k)x
$$
We derive the following expression:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d(x^2)}{dk}&=\frac{dx^2}{dt}\cdot \frac{dt}{dk}=2x\cdot \dot{x}\cdot \frac{1}{\dot{k}} \
&=2x(a+bk^2\cos k)x\cdot \frac{1}{x^2}=2(a+bk^2\cos k)
\end{aligned}
$$
Integrating both sides of (88) from $k(t_0)$ to $k(t)$:
$$
x^2(k(t))-x^2(k(t_0))=2\int_{k(t_0)}^{k(t)}(a+b\tau^2\cos \tau)d\tau \
=(\tau^2\sin\tau+2\tau\cos\tau-2\sin\tau)\Bigg|^{k(t)}_{k(t_0)}
$$
Thus we have:
$$
x^2(k(t))=x^2(k(t_0))+2\phi(k(t))-2\phi(k(t_0))
$$
where $\phi(k(t))=ak(t)+b(k^2\sin k(t)+2k\cos k-2\sin k)$

($x\in\mathcal{L}_\infty$ and $\dot{x}\in\mathcal{L}_\infty \implies x\rightarrow 0$)

Note that $k(t)$ is monotone nondecreasing. $k(t)$ must either approach a finite limit or grow without bound. If $k(t)$ grows without bound, the sign of $\phi(k(t))$ will be dominated by the term $bk^2(t)\sin k(t)$, which can assume a large negative value independent of the sign of $b$. As a result , the right side of (90) is negative. Clearly, there is a contradiction with $x^2(k(t))\ge 0$. Therefore, $k(t)$ must be bounded. Then $x\in\mathcal{L}_\infty$ from (90) and $x\in\mathcal{L}_2$ from $\dot{k}=x^2$. From (87) we known $\dot{x}\in\mathcal{L}\infty$. From Barbalat’s Lemma, $\lim \limits{t\rightarrow\infty}=0$.

Robust Adaptive Control

Parameter Drift

Our starting point is the following scalar system:
$$
\dot{x}=u+\phi(x)\theta+d(t)
$$
where $x$ is the state, $u$ is the control input, $\phi$ is a continuous known function, $\theta$ is the unknown constant, and $d(t)$ is a bounded time-dependent disturbance with $\vert d(t)\vert \le d_{max}$.

Control Objective: Design $u$, such that all signals in the closed-loop system are bounded, and the state $x$ is derived to zero. (or to a small neighbor of zero)

When $d(t)\equiv 0$, we proposed the following control input
$$
u=-\phi(x)\hat{\theta}(t)-kx
$$
The the closed-loop system is :
$$
\dot{x}=-kx-\phi(x)\tilde{\theta},\ \tilde{\theta}=\hat{\theta}-\theta
$$
Consider a Lyapunov function candidate:
$$
\dot{V}=\frac{1}{2}x^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma}\tilde{\theta}^2
$$
Its derivative along (93) is :
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &= x\dot{x}+\frac{1}{\gamma}\tilde{\theta}\dot{\hat{\theta}}\
&=-kx^2-x\phi(x)\tilde{\theta}+\frac{1}{\gamma}\tilde{\theta}\dot{\hat{\theta}} \
&=-kx^2+\frac{1}{\gamma}\tilde{\theta}(\dot{\hat{\theta}}-\gamma x\phi(x))
\end{aligned}
$$
Then the adaptation law can be designed as:
$$
\dot{\hat{\theta}}=\gamma x\phi(x)
$$
But when $d(t)\ne0$, the closed-loop system is:
$$
\dot{x}=-kx-\phi(x)\tilde{\theta}+d(t)
$$
The derivative of $V$ in (94) along (97) is:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &= -kx^2+\gamma d(t) \
&\le -kx^2+d_{max}\vert x\vert \
&=-k\vert x\vert(\vert x\vert-\frac{d_{max}}{k})
\end{aligned}
$$
Clearly, $\dot{V} \le0$ outside the set $E_0=\left{(x,\tilde{\theta}):\Vert x\Vert \le\frac{d_{max}}{k}:=e_0 \right}$. Inside $E_0$, $\dot{V}$ might be positive, and as a consequence, the parameter error $\vert\tilde{\theta}(t)\vert$ can grow freely and even become unbounded. This phenomenon is known as parameter drift. It is caused by the disturbance. This is argument shows that the proposed adaptive law is not robust.

The Dead-Zone Modification

In order to enforce robustness, we consider an adaptive law with the dead-zone modification:
$$
\dot{\hat{\theta}}=
\left{
\begin{aligned}
\gamma &x\phi(x),&if \Vert x\Vert\ge e_0+\delta \
&0,&if \Vert x\Vert\lt e_0+\delta
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$
Remind $V=\frac{1}{2}x^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma}\tilde{\theta}^2$. and $\dot{V}\le -k\Vert x\Vert(\Vert x\Vert-e_0))$. And $e_0\triangleq \frac{d_{max}}{k}$. Then we define:
$$
n_1 \triangleq \left{t:\Vert x(t)\Vert\lt e_0+\delta \right} \
n_2 \triangleq \left{t:\Vert x(t)\Vert \ge e_0+\delta \right}
$$

If $t_0\in n_1$, and the trajectory reaches the boundary of $n_1$, and is outside at $(t_1,t_2)\in n_2$, we have:
$$
V(t)=\frac{1}{2}(e_0+\delta)^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma}\tilde{\theta}^2(t_0) \
V(t)=\frac{1}{2}(e_0+\delta)^2+\frac{1}{2\gamma}\tilde\theta^2(t_2)\lt V(t_1)
$$
When $t\in n_2$, $\dot{V}\le-k(e_0+\delta)\cdot\delta\triangleq-c,\ c\gt0$. We can conclude that $\Vert \tilde{\theta}(t)\Vert$ at $t_1,t_2,t_3,\cdots$ is monotonously decreasing. Finally, we can get $\Vert \tilde{\theta}(t)\Vert$ is bounded, and $\Vert x\Vert\le e_0+\delta$.

Advantage of Dead-Zone Modification: Simplicity and effectiveness

Drawbacks:

  1. The assumption that an upper bound for the disturbances need is known a priori
  2. In the absence of disturbance, asymptotic stability of the tracking error cannot be recovered. In other words, robustness is achieved at the expense of destroying some of the ideal properties of the adaptive law.
The $\sigma$-Modification

The $\sigma$-modification scheme, developed by Ioanon and Kokotovic does not require any priori information on the disturbance upper bounds.

The adaptive law with the $\sigma$-modification is (with the ideal of feedback control) :
$$
u=-\phi(x)\hat{\theta}(t)-kx \
\dot{\hat{\theta}}=\gamma(x\phi(x)-\sigma\hat{\theta})
$$
where $\sigma$ and $\gamma$ are positive constants. In essence, this modification adds some damping to the ideal adaptive law. The motivation here is to prevent parameter drift. Under (105), the derivative of (94) along (93) is :
$$
\dot{V}=-kx^2-x\phi(x)\tilde{\theta}+xd(t)+\tilde{\theta}(x\phi(x)-\sigma\hat{\theta}) =-kx^2+xd(t)-\sigma\tilde{\theta}\hat{\theta}
$$
Note that:
$$
xd(t)\le d_{max}\Vert x\Vert\le\frac{k}{2}x^2+\frac{1}{2k}d^2_{max}
$$
and:
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\sigma\tilde{\theta}\hat{\theta} &=
-\sigma\tilde{\theta}(\tilde\theta+\theta) \
&=-\sigma\tilde\theta^2-\sigma\tilde\theta\theta \
&\le-\sigma\tilde{\theta}^2+\frac{\sigma}{2}(\tilde\theta^2+\theta^2) \
&=-\frac{\sigma}{2}\tilde\theta^2+\frac{\sigma\theta^2}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Substituting (108) and (107) into (106) yields:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{V} &\le -kx^2+\frac{k}{2}x^2+\frac{d^2_{max}}{2k}
-\frac{\sigma}{2}\tilde{\theta}^2+\frac{\sigma\theta^2}{2} \
&=-\frac{k}{2}x^2-\frac{\sigma}{2}\tilde{\theta}^2+\frac{d^2_{max}}{2k}
+\frac{\sigma\theta^2}{2} \
&=-k\cdot\frac{1}{2}-\sigma\gamma\cdot\frac{1}{2\gamma}\tilde{\theta}^2+C \
&\le -\min{k,\sigma\gamma}V+C \triangleq -\beta V+C
\end{aligned}
$$
We have $V(t)\le V(0)\cdot e^{-\beta t}+\frac{C}{\beta}(1-e^{-\beta t})$. By adjusting $\sigma$, the convergence rate of $V(t)$ can be modified.
